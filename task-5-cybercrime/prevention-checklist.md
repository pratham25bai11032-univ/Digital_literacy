# Stay Safe Online — A Checklist for College Students in India

*Put together as part of a Digital Literacy assignment, but honestly useful
regardless of that.*

> If something has already gone wrong: **call 1930** (National Cyber Crime
> Helpline) or file a complaint at **cybercrime.gov.in** — do it fast,
> especially for money-related fraud.

---

## A Quick Note Before the List

Most of us think we are already careful online. I thought the same thing until
I started researching for this assignment and realised how many of these habits
I was not actually following. This is not a list of things only "non-tech"
people need to read. Some of these tripped up people who use the internet all
day, every day. So here it is — no jargon, no corporate tone, just things that
actually matter for someone living in a hostel, buying and selling stuff online,
applying for internships, and doing their banking on a phone.

---

## 🔐 Passwords and Account Security

- [ ] **Stop reusing the same passwords everywhere.**
  I know it is inconvenient. But if one platform you use gets hacked and your
  password leaks — which happens more often than you think — every other account
  with that same password is now compromised too. Use a password manager like
  Google Password Manager. You only need to remember one
  master password and it handles the rest.

- [ ] **Turn on Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) on everything important.**
  Email, Instagram, your college portal, your UPI apps — all of it.It
  adds one extra step when you log in. That extra step is also the thing
  standing between a stranger and your account if your password ever gets
  leaked. An authenticator app (Google Authenticator or Authy) is more secure
  than SMS-based OTP, but even SMS 2FA is better than nothing.

- [ ] **Update your apps and phone OS when the notification shows up.**
  I know we all tap "remind me later" and forget about it for six months. Those
  updates often contain security patches for vulnerabilities that attackers are
  actively exploiting. Delaying them is a genuinely bad idea.
---

## 📶 Wi-Fi and Public Networks

- [ ] **Do not use public Wi-Fi for anything sensitive.**
  The free Wi-Fi at a cafe, bus stand, or mall is not a safe places to log into
  your bank, check your college portal, or enter any password. These networks
  are often unencrypted, meaning someone else on the same network can
  potentially see your traffic. Use your mobile data for anything that matters.
  If you have to use public Wi-Fi regularly, look into a basic VPN.

---

## 📱 App Permissions

- [ ] **Check what permissions your apps actually have.**
  Go to Settings → Apps → Permissions on your phone right now and have a look.
  You will probably find at least one app with access to things it has no
  business having — your location, your contacts, your microphone. A notes app
  does not need your camera. A weather app does not need your contacts. Revoke
  anything that does not make obvious sense.

---

## 💰 UPI and Financial Safety — Read This Twice

These two are the most important ones on this entire list. If you take nothing
else from this checklist, take these.

- [ ] **You never need to enter your UPI PIN to receive money. Ever.**
  This is the single most exploited gap in UPI knowledge in India right now.
  When someone sends you a collect request — which looks like a payment
  notification and shows an amount — entering your PIN does not confirm receipt.
  It authorises an outgoing transfer from your account. If anyone on a call,
  over chat, or in any message tells you to "enter your PIN to receive the
  payment," stop immediately. That is the scam. Hang up, close the app, and
  do not proceed.

- [ ] **Never share your UPI PIN, OTP, or card CVV with anyone.**
  Not with someone claiming to be from your bank. Not from "NPCI customer
  support." Not from a Google Pay helpline. Not from anyone. Legitimate banks
  and payment platforms will never call you and ask for these. If someone is
  asking, they are not who they say they are. End the call.

- [ ] **Before you approve any payment request, actually read what it says.**
  Does it say "Pay" or "Receive"? These words are there in the app — we just
  do not read them because we are in a hurry or someone on a call is talking
  over us and telling us what to do. Slow down for five seconds. Read the
  actual text on the screen before entering your PIN.

- [ ] **Set a daily UPI transaction limit in your bank app.**
  Most banks let you do this. It takes two minutes. If your account is ever
  compromised, a lower limit caps how much damage can be done in a single
  session. Think of it as a speed bump for fraud — it will not stop everything,
  but it limits your worst-case scenario.

## 🌐 Social Media and Your Digital Footprint

- [ ] **Make your social media profiles private and actually audit them.**
  Not just flipping the private toggle and forgetting about it, but going
  through your followers list and removing people you do not know. Your daily
  routine, your location, your college ID, your phone number — this information
  is genuinely useful to people who want to scam or harass you, and a lot of
  us post it without thinking twice.

- [ ] **Before you post something, ask yourself if you would be okay with
  everyone seeing it permanently.**
  Screenshots are forever. That "private" group chat can be screenshotted and
  shared. That photo from the trip can resurface years later. Future employers,
  internship coordinators, and professors do look at social media. You do not
  have to be boring online, just intentional.

- [ ] **Be very skeptical of new accounts that reach out to you out of nowhere.**
  A profile created three months ago with 12 posts offering you a paid internship
  is a red flag. A stranger who sends a friend request and immediately starts a
  conversation asking personal questions is a red flag. Trust your instincts if it feels off, it probably is.

---

## 📧 Phishing and Scam Links

- [ ] **Look at the actual email address, not just the display name.**
  An email can show "SBI Bank Support" as the sender name while the actual
  address is something like sbi-alert@helpdesk-verify.net. The part after the
  @ symbol is what tells you where it actually came from. If the domain looks
  weird or unfamiliar, do not click anything in that email.

- [ ] **Do not click links sent over WhatsApp, Instagram, or SMS from
  unknown numbers — even if the message looks urgent.**
  "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours." "You have won a prize, claim
  now." "Verify your KYC immediately." These are all designed to make you act
  before you think. If something is genuinely urgent from a real organisation,
  you can always go directly to their official website or app instead of
  following a random link.

- [ ] **Verify internship and job offers by going directly to the company's
  official website.**
  If you get an email saying you have been selected for an internship, do not
  just click the link in the email. Search the company name yourself, go to
  their official site, and see if the opportunity actually exists. Legitimate
  companies do not ask for a "registration fee" or "security deposit" as part
  of their application process. If they do, it is not a real company offering
  you a real internship.

---

## 🚨 If Something Goes Wrong — Report It

A lot of people do not report cybercrime because they feel embarrassed, or they
think nothing will come of it. Both of those feelings are understandable and
both of them end up helping the fraudsters more than anyone else.

Here is what to do and where to go:

| What To Do | Where |
|---|---|
| **Report online** | cybercrime.gov.in — works for financial fraud, harassment, social media abuse, everything |
| **Call the helpline** | **1930** — do this immediately for money-related fraud, the faster the better |
| **Contact your bank** | Call your bank's fraud helpline and ask them to flag or freeze the transaction |
| **File an FIR** | Your nearest police station or cyber crime cell — creates an official record |

One thing worth knowing about calling 1930: the earlier you call after a
fraudulent UPI transaction, the higher the chance that the transferred amount
can be flagged or frozen before it is withdrawn on the other end. Every hour
you wait reduces that chance. So if it happens, call first and feel embarrassed
later.

---

## Final Thought

Going through all of this for the assignment, the thing that stayed with me
is that almost none of these require technical knowledge. You do not need to
understand how encryption works or what a VPN protocol does. You just need a
few specific habits — and the willingness to slow down for five seconds before
doing something that feels urgent.

Most of the time, that five-second pause is all the difference between safe
and scammed.
