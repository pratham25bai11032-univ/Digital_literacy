## Introduction

Out of all the cybercrimes happening in India right now, The UPI fraud is probably
the one that most likely to hit a college student life. We use PhonePe, Google Pay, and
Paytm for almost everything ,splitting mess bills, paying for notes,sending
money home. It feels completely normal and safe because we do it every single
day. That normalacy is exactly what makes this type of fraud type so dangerous.
Attackers are not targeting people who have never used UPI. They are targeting
people who think they already know how it works and fool them.

I chose this topic because I personally know someone in my hostel who lost
₹8,000 to this exact scam during our second semester. He was not careless or
uneducated. He just did not know one specific thing about how UPI collect
requests work and that one gap cost him real money.
---

## What Is UPI Payment Fraud?

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) fraud is a type of financial cybercrime where
the attacker manipulates the victim into either approving a money transfer
they did not intend to make, or handing over sensitive credentials like their
UPI PIN or OTP. The attack usually involves social engineering, meaning the
criminal is not hacking any system. They are hacking/manipulating the person.

What makes UPI fraud particularly tricky is that the platform itself is
legitimate and well-designed and used everyday. The fraud does not happen because the app is
broken. It happens in the gap between what the user thinks they are doing
and what they are actually doing. And that gap, in many cases, comes down
to one misunderstood button.

## The Scenario => Step by Step

Here is a realistic account based on a pattern reported across hundreds of
cases in India:

**Ravi, a 20-year-old student**, posted his old laptop on OLX for
₹22,000. Within a few hours, he got a call from someone who introduced himself
as "Captain Arvind Singh" , an army officer who needed the laptop urgently
before a transfer posting. The story sounded completely believable. The caller
was polite, spoke confidently, and did not try to negotiate the price at all,
which actually made the Ravi trust him more and scam began therre. Why would a scammer not even try to
bargain?

**Step 1 — Building trust:**
The caller said he would pay the full amount right away and asked Ravi to share
his UPI ID. Ravi gave it without hesitation. Why would he not? He was the one
supposed to be receiving money.

**Step 2 — Sending the collect request:**
A few seconds later, Ravi got a notification on his PhonePe app. It showed
₹22,000. The caller said, "I've sent you the money, just approve it from your
side."

**Step 3 — The critical mistake:**
Ravi opened the notification, saw the amount, and entered his UPI PIN to
"confirm receipt." He genuinely believed he was accepting an incoming payment.
What he actually did was approve an outgoing collect request — giving PhonePe
permission to pull ₹22,000 out of his account and send it directly to the
attacker.

**Step 4 — Realisation:**
Within seconds, Ravis phone buzzed: "Debited ₹22,000." The caller had already
disconnected. The number was switched off before Ravi could even hit redial.

The entire thing took only ten minutes.

## Why This Works — The Psychology Behind It

This is the part I find most interesting, and honestly a bit unsettling.

The attacker did not need any technical skill nor any hacking, no malware, no
fake website. They just needed to know one thing: most people do not know that
entering your UPI PIN on a collect request sends money out of your account,
not into it. The UPI interface does not make this obvious. The notification
shows the amount, the app looks familiar, and the caller is telling you it
is a payment coming your way. Every signal points in the wrong direction.

Add to that the urgency the caller created ("I need this before tomorrow
morning"), the emotional credibility of an army officer persona, and Ravi
being genuinely excited about finally selling his laptop and his critical
thinking was essentially offline at the exact moment he needed it most.

This is textbook social engineering. It works not because users are dumb, but
because it is specifically designed to exploit the way normal human beings
think under excitement and time pressure. That distinction matters.

## Who Gets Targeted?

Based on reported cases and news coverage, the most common targets include:

- **Students selling second-hand items** on OLX, Quikr, 
- **People using UPI frequently but without deep familiarity** with features
  like collect requests — which sounds contradictory but is actually very common
- **Anyone in a hurry to complete a transaction** — urgency lowers scrutiny
- **Elderly users** introduced to UPI post-demonetisation without detailed
  training on how specific features work
- **Small shopkeepers and daily wage earners** who use UPI constantly but
  never had the collect request mechanic explained to them

## Consequences

**For the victim:**

The financial loss is immediate and, in most cases , permanent. UPI transactions
settle in real time — the money moves the instant the PIN is entered. By the
time the victim calls their bank, the funds are often already gone. Even with
a prompt complaint, recovery rates for UPI fraud in India remain very low.

Beyond the money, there is a psychological cost that rarely gets talked about.
Ravi said he felt more embarrassed than angry — like he should have known
better. That shame made him wait two days before reporting it, which only
reduced his already slim chances of recovering anything. A lot of victims
never report at all, which means the real scale of this problem is probably
much larger than official numbers suggest.

**Legal consequences for the attacker:**

Under Indian law, this kind of fraud is covered by:
- **Section 66C of the IT Act, 2000** — identity theft through a false persona
- **Section 66D of the IT Act, 2000** — cheating by impersonation using
  computer resources
- **Section 420 of the IPC** — cheating and dishonestly inducing delivery
  of property

These carry penalties of up to 3 years imprisonment and/or a fine. In practice,
conviction rates are low because attackers use burner SIM cards, VoIP numbers,
and layered mule bank accounts that are difficult to trace in time.

**Broader impact:**

Every successful UPI fraud chips away at public trust in digital payments —
which is a real problem in a country that is actively building a cashless
economy. NPCI and government agencies now spend significant resources on
awareness campaigns specifically because of how widespread these incidents have
become.

## What Could Have Prevented This?

Honestly, just one piece of knowledge would have been enough: **you never need
to enter your UPI PIN to receive money.** That is it. If anyone — no matter
how legitimate they sound — asks you to enter your PIN or approve something
to receive a payment, stop. That is the scam, every single time.

A few other habits that would have helped Ravi specifically:

- Read the full notification before acting. It will say "Pay" or "Collect" —
  those words are right there. We just do not read them because the caller is
  talking in our ear telling us what to do.
- If something feels rushed, that is not a sign to move faster — it is a
  signal to slow down. Legitimate buyers do not need you to approve a payment
  in the next 30 seconds.
- Never share your UPI ID in response to an inbound call. Ask the buyer to
  contact you through the platform listing instead.
- Set a daily UPI limit below your account balance so that even if you are
  tricked, the maximum possible loss is capped.

---

## Reporting

If this happens to you or someone you know, act immediately:

1. **Call 1930** — the National Cyber Crime Helpline. The faster you call,
   the better the chance of freezing the transaction before the money
   is withdrawn from the other end.
2. **File a complaint at cybercrime.gov.in** — keep your transaction ID,
   the fraudster's number, and any screenshots ready before you start.
3. **Contact your bank's fraud helpline** and request a transaction dispute.
4. **File an FIR** at your nearest cyber crime police station to create an
   official record that can support any future recovery attempt.

And please do not delay because of embarrassment. These are organised criminal
operations running this same script on thousands of people across India. You
reporting it adds to a data trail that actually helps law enforcement build
cases over time.

## My Takeaway

Writing this made me realise how thin the line is between being digitally
active and being digitally safe. Ravi used UPI every day. He was not naive.
He just had one blind spot, and someone found it.

The uncomfortable part is that this scam is not sophisticated at all. It
needs no coding, no hacking, no fake website. Just a phone call and the
assumption that the person on the other end does not know what a collect
request does. Most people do not — until they learn the hard way.

That feels like something a Digital Literacy course should fix before the
scammer does.
